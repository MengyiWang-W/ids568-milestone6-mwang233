Claim 1:
RAG significantly reduces hallucination because it grounds model outputs in retrieved factual documents.

Claim 2:
RAG does not eliminate hallucination entirely, especially when retrieval fails or retrieves irrelevant documents.

Supporting Argument:
Even when relevant documents exist in the corpus, poor chunking or similarity search errors can cause the system to retrieve incorrect context.

Example:
If a query is ambiguous, the retriever may select semantically similar but incorrect chunks, leading to misleading answers.

Contradiction:
Some studies claim that increasing top-k always improves performance, while others show that larger k introduces noise and reduces answer quality.

Edge Case:
When no relevant information exists in the corpus, the model may still generate an answer instead of admitting uncertainty.

Implication:
The effectiveness of RAG depends heavily on retrieval quality, chunking strategy, and prompt design rather than the LLM alone.
